Pune: The Centre has withdrawn 14 BIS quality
control orders (QCOs) covering key chemical, poly-
mer and fibre-based materials, which are used to
make finished goods such as garments, fertilisers
and several chemicals, simplifying the process of
their imports and making them cheaper .
The QCOs, which are compulsory certifications
given by the Bureau of  Indian Standards (BIS), in-
creased compliance costs for small businesses, lea-
ding to higher prices for consumers and reduced
competitiveness, especially for MSMEs and ex-
port-oriented industries.
The rescinding of  QCOs indicates a major shift to-
wards industry liberalisation and reduced regula-
tory burden in a major relief  to the chemical, plas-
tics and textile sectors. Globally , about 70% of  the
garments are made using synthetic fibre, and Indi-
an exporters were unable to compete as they were
forced to buy expensive domestically produced po-
lyester fibre and yarn.
The chemicals and fertilisers ministry removed
the QCOs on Wednesday , a move that would make
the Chinese polyester fibre available to Indian gar-
ment and fabric manufacturers at 20-25% lower ra-
tes than the domestic polyester , said industry ex-
ecutives. The move will ensure raw material availa-
bility , ease import constraints and lower input
costs for downstream MSMEs in the packaging,
textile and moulding sectors.
“The decision of  rescinding QCO on polyester fi-
bre and yarn is a big move to make India competiti-
ve in man-made fibre, which has 70% market share.
With the already bagged FTAs (free trade agree-
ments) and Europe negotiations on fast track, In-
dia looks set to become a global leader in apparel
and textiles,” said Sanjay Jain, former chairman
of  the Confederation of  Indian Textile Industry .
The Southern India Mills’ Association said the
initiative will help prevent the closure of  many po-
wer loom units that are on the verge of  becoming
non-performing assets due to non-availability and
shortage of  required fibres at competitive prices. 
Its chairman, Durai Palanisamy , said, “Many do-
mestic manufacturers of  comfort wear and techni-
cal textile products had been struggling to procure
fibres at globally competitive rates, resulting in a
loss of  market share built over decades.
